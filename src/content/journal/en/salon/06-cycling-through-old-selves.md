I rode past my primary school, middle school, high school, the first and second companies where I interned. I rode past the first bar my father took me to, to watch the 2014 World Cup; past the FamilyMarts I had entered so many times I no longer needed to look up; past the road where, in senior year, I walked my best friend home every day.

Wind lifted the loose pieces of my hair and the past began to flash—jump-cut, frame by frame, like a roll of film. The girl in middle school who believed in the outside world. The girl in high school who had no idea where her future lived. The girl in college, sleeping five hours a night and always in transit. Speeding down the street felt like time travel: like falling into the world's kaleidoscope.

Now I cycle a shared bike through Shanghai's plane-tree streets at night, watching everything as an outsider. Perhaps I, too, have become a Jiangsu person constructed by society—*subo live matter!* Between me and the world there is a gauze-thin layer. The city is noisy, restless, and full of human warmth. I can be cool and solitary here, and still love it from somewhere very deep.
