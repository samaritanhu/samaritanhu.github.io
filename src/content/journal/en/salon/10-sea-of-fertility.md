For one instant, the vast universe seemed to acquire the force of eternity. When I closed the book, the four volumes of *The Sea of Fertility* had arrived at their unavoidable ending.

Before opening *The Decay of the Angel*, I delayed for as long as I could. I loved Mishima's work too much to leave it. I read around its edges: returned to *Thirst for Love*; read his lectures on immorality and new love. Before this late summation, they all seemed like fragments that no longer knew what they were pointing toward.

Kiyoaki's white death for love. Isao's stallion-bright death for an ideal. The princess's death of the body. Tōru's death inside an evil, wounded pride. Four reincarnations. Only at the end does Honda seem able to lay down money and looking, those things he once called reason; to leave behind the loudness and fuss of the ordinary world, and return to Gesshū Temple—to a place with neither memory nor a single thing.

The heat of being eighteen, the obsessions of being eighteen, those burning summers and spilled blood: on the running line of our lives they flash once like afterglow, then pull a bright tail behind them and vanish toward some corner of the sky we do not know.

Maybe after the hundredth, the ten-thousandth, or the hundred-millionth rebirth, you and I will meet somewhere again.

Thank you.
